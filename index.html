How a sighting happens

Every dot on the map began with a person who stopped to look

1

Someone spots it

A birder with a long lens, a hiker, a parent at the playground, a ranger on rounds. Anyone. Most records here come from ordinary New Yorkers, not scientists.

2

They log it

They photograph the animal and post it to iNaturalist, a free app run by the California Academy of Sciences and the National Geographic Society. The phone stamps the time and place automatically.

3

The crowd checks it

Other naturalists anywhere in the world review the photo and add their own identification, confirming the species or correcting a wrong guess.

4

It becomes a record

Once at least two-thirds agree on the species, with a photo and a date attached, the sighting turns “research grade” and lands on this map. Everything here has cleared that bar.

The rescue log further down works differently. Those come from the New York City Urban Park Rangers, the uniformed staff who respond when someone reports an animal in a park that is hurt, stranded or out of place. A ranger records the species, its condition and what happened next.

Where this comes from

Species counts, the map and the notable feed are built from iNaturalist, restricted to research-grade observations inside the New York City boundary (place 674). Research grade means at least two-thirds of identifiers agreed on the species, with a photo and a date. The rescue records come from NYC Open Data, the Urban Park Rangers' animal-condition response log.

A few honest limits. Counts reflect where people look, not where animals are: a well-birded park will out-report a quiet one. “Notable” here means rarely reported, which can mean genuinely rare, hard to spot, or simply new to the city. Escaped pets and one-off strays show up alongside true wild residents. Records logged at a zoo or aquarium are dropped from the map and the notable feed, since captive animals often are not marked as such. And the ubiquitous species you asked to set aside (pigeon, house sparrow, starling, brown and black rat, house mouse, feral cat and dog) are flagged and hidden by default, never dropped from the record.

This page is assembled automatically from public APIs and refreshed daily. Records are only as good as the observers and identifiers who made them; misidentifications, joke entries and location fuzzing (iNaturalist obscures the exact spot of sensitive or endangered species) all exist in the source data. Treat individual points as leads, not proof. Nothing here is edited or interpreted by hand.
